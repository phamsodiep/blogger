Scrum is an agile framework for developing, delivering, and sustaining complex products. It is designed for teams of ten or fewer members, who break their work into goals that can be completed within timeboxed iterations. The Scrum Team track progress in 15-minute time-boxed daily meetings. At the end of a iteration, the team holds review and retrospective to continuously improve.
Audience: software engineer – Topic: project management framework

Introduction:
"Problems are inevitable, Problems are soluble.", that is claimed by David Deutsch. Problems are inevitable, because our knowledge will always be infinitely far from complete. Problems are soluble, and each particular evil is a problem that can be solved. Let's investigate problems and knowledge in some following software methodologies.
1. Waterfall model
For waterfall methodology, software requirements (Figure 1, Requirements of the waterfall model) are analyzed by a Business Analyzer captured in a product requirements document. But problems are inevitable event if Business Analyzer does the best works because:
Customer incorrectly describes the requirement.
Business Analyzer incorrectly captures requirement because of complexity nature.
Team and technology could not implement the requirement as they expect.
Communication problems cause knowledge sharing is not good. Business Analyzer captures requirement correctly, but team understand incorrectly.
... (you could add more from your practice)
The more complex requirements, the more potential problems are arisen. The key to solve the problems is knowledge. But what knowledge really is? Karl Popper disagrees that knowledge is collection of observations, nor with the invention of experiments. The reason is that observations and experiments are biased. Knowledge is conjecture alternating with criticism. Such knowledge is archived after Verification of the waterfall model (Figure 1). This means that we have knowledge after the product is released. There are two type of knowledge:
Knowledge about the target implemented system
Knowledge about the technology implements the system
Waterfall methodology is suitable for case that such knowledge is straightforward (where requirements and scope are fixed, the product itself is firm and stable, and the technology is clearly understood.).
2. Iterative incremental model
To support team archive knowledge, iterative incremental model is introduced. The requirements are broken down and they are being evoluted. They are added over iterations. An iteration is similar to waterfall model, it releases an outcome called as Software Product Increment. The requirements are broken down and evoluted so that the knowledge required for the early iteration is straightforward. After each iteration, we "earn" more knowledge that is ready for next requirement evolution. We base on the Software Product Increment to define the next Requirements (select which pending requirement to put into next iteration implementation list, add new requirement or modify an existing requirement).
The outcome of the subsequent iteration is an enhanced working increment of the product. This is repeated till the product accomplishes the required functionalities.

II. Agile methodology

1. Introduction
Iterative incremental model assumes that customer could correctly describe the requirement. The requirement is not expected to be changed. However, it is common that requirement is changed. Agile methodology encourages this. It is based on iterative incremental model with below characteristics:
Requirements and solutions evolve through team collaboration.
Time-boxed iterative approach, encourages rapid and flexible response to change.
2. Implemented values
As the Agile Manifesto:
"We are uncovering better ways of developing software by doing it and helping others do it. Through this work, we have come to value:
Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
Working software over comprehensive documentation
Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
Responding to change over following a plan
That is, while there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more."
Manifesto for Agile Software Development, Authors: Beck, Kent, et al. (2001)
The implemented values are: individuals and interactions, working software, customer collaboration, responding to change. Detail descriptions is as below.
Individuals and interactions: self-organization, self-motivation of the team members and continuous interaction for work, clarifications, information among the team members.
Working software: delivery of working software at short duration intervals helps gain customer trust and assurance in the team.
Customer collaboration: constant involvement of customer with the development team ensures communication of necessary modifications.
Responding to change: focus on quick response to the proposed changes, which is made possible with short duration iterations.
The key element of Agile Manifesto is that we must trust people and their ability to collaborate.
3. Principles
The Agile Manifesto is based on the following principles:
Satisfaction and Delivery: customer satisfaction through early and continuous working software.
Welcoming Change: welcome changing requirements, even at later stages of development.
Deliver Frequently: deliver working software frequently (weekly rather than monthly).
Communication is the Key: ensure close association of developers with business people on daily basis.
Environment and Trust: build projects around motivated individuals. Give them necessary support and trust them.
Face-to-face Communication: encourage face-to-face conversation to ensure efficient and effective communication.
Software as Measure of Progress: working software is the primary measure of progress.
Sustainable Development: promote sustainable development with the ability to maintain a constant pace throughout the development.
Attention to Details: continuous attention to technical excellence and good design.
The Power of Less: simplicity is essential.
Self-organizing Teams: regular attention of the team on becoming effective in changing circumstances.
4. How this works
Base on the characteristics of Agile methodology, its benefits are:
1. Faster Time to Deliver/ Market
2. Reduce Uncertainty and Risk
3. Increase Return on Investment (ROI) by focusing on Customer Value
We shall analyze how these benefits are archived.
Concepts
Paradigm: paradigm is a distinct set of concepts or thought patterns, including theories, research methods, postulates, and standards for what constitutes legitimate contributions to a field.
Methodology: methodology is the systematic, theoretical analysis of the methods applied to a field of study.
Framework: framework is a partial solution for a problem that the solution could be customized or filled in to complete it. The framework solves a class of problems, it need customization or fill for a specific problem.
Paradigm
To archive target benefits, the Agile methodology proposes:
1. Let product evolute
2. Let team's knowledge evolve
3. Let customer join the development team and let they learn more knowledge about the implemented system so that they could change their requirement which affect on #1.
The paradigm for #1 is dialectical materialism and for #2, #3 is knowledge evolution (David Deutsch). For optimization, Synergy also is applied.
Methodology
We shall analyze how paradigm is applied to propose methodology principles.
Software product is implemented for customer business values. Because of value additivity nature, we could partition the software product into software product incremental so that the early values archivement is meanfull to customer for principle Satisfaction and Delivery. The software product incremental is so small for Deliver Frequently, The Power of Less and Sustainable Development principles. Welcoming Change principle could be archived by view change as software product incremental. With time-boxed iterative approach, a software product incremental could be treated as a granular unit for measurement, so principle Software as Measure of Progress could be archived.
These implement the benefit #1. Faster Time to Deliver/ Market. The The Power of Less principle implement the benefit #2. Reduce Uncertainty and Risk. By welcoming change, we focus on customer value rather than requirements for benefit #3. Increase Return on Investment (ROI) by focusing on Customer Value. This could be implemented if both customer and team members aware about the values and requirements.
We need a solution to implement knowledge sharing and to maximize each member potential skill (Syngegy approach). This results in following principles: Self-organizing Teams, Communication is the Key, Environment and Trust, Face-to-face Communication, Attention to Details.

III. Scrum framework

1. Overview
Agile is a methodology, while Scrum is a framework. It is a partial solution for complex adaptive problems, while productively and creatively delivering products of the highest possible value.
The Scrum framework consists of Scrum Teams and their associated roles, events, artifacts, and rules. Each component within the framework serves a specific purpose and is essential to Scrum’s success and usage.
The rules of Scrum bind together the events, roles, and artifacts, governing the relationships and interaction between them.
2. How it is implemented?
We shall investigate how Agile principles are applied and implemented in Scrum:
Requirement:
The requirements are modeled and partitioned into User Story. Each User Story then could be partitioned further to smaller User Stories. The User Story is written from customer view or perspective regard to business value. The product requirements are modeled as Product Backlog which is the list of all User Story.
A software product incremental is implemented in a time-box duration called sprint. The requirements for that sprint named as Sprint Backlog which is a subset of Product Backlog.
Roles:
Team: members that implements the product.
ScrumMaster: this person is the keeper of Scrum process.
Product Owner: on behalf of customer.
Implementing: Agile methodology is implemented in Scrum as a process composed by a sequence of time-boxed events following its rules.
Events:
The Sprint: product increment developing
Sprint Planning: define next increment scope and how to do them
Daily Scrum Meeting: understand the previous day work and plan for next day
Sprint Review: understand the previous sprint done (feedback)
Sprint Retrospective: introspect and improve (lesson learn)
Rules:
Time-boxed base duration: Scrum process is composed by events not phases because of the time-boxed base nature. This ensures granular activities which then also supports granular unit measurement.
The Intention of Every Sprint is "Potentially Shippable" Software: focus on customer values.
Every Sprint includes Sprint Planning: outcome of previous incremental is taken into account for this sprint planning. This means that we are learning from work (by doing) instead of investigate or analyzing.
Every Sprint includes Sprint Review: for customer feedback on the product.
Every Sprint includes Sprint Retrospective: for the team to inspect and adapt
3. Process framework
Events:
Sprint:
Activities: develop a working product increment.
Duration: Fixed for all sprints (two weeks or one month)
Inputs: Sprint Goal (Sprint Planning outcome)
Outputs: Working product Increment
Rules:
There is no break between sprint.
Sprint should be cancelled if the Sprint Goal becomes obsolete (by Product Owner only). Incomplete Sprint Backlog Items are put back into the Product Backlog. Part of the work produced during the sprint is potentially releasable.
Daily Scrum Meetings:
Activities: understanding previous day works to plan the next day works. Team member explains:
What did she or he do yesterday that helped the Team meet the Sprint Goal?
What will she or he do today to help the Team meet the Sprint Goal?
Does she or he see any impediments that prevent team from meeting the Sprint Goal?
Duration: 15 minutes
Inputs: how the team is doing toward meeting the Sprint Goal.
Outputs: a new or revised plan that optimizes the team’s efforts in meeting the Sprint Goal.
Rules:
Re-plan the rest of the Sprint’s work could be conducte if there are impediments.
Sprint Review:
Activities: Obtain feedback and progress unitedly. Scrum Team and the stakeholders collaborate to understand what was done in the Sprint. Steps are:
The Product Owner explains what Product Backlog items have been completed during the sprint and what has not been completed.
The Team discusses what went well during the Sprint, what problems it ran into, and how those problems were solved.
The Team demonstrates the work that it has completed and answers questions, if any, about the Increment.
The entire group then discusses on what to do next.
The Scrum Team then reviews the timeline, budget, potential capabilities, and marketplace for the next anticipated release of the product increment.
The outcome of the Sprint Review is an updated Product Backlog, which defines the probable Product Backlog items for the next Sprint.
Duration: two hours for two week sprints and for four hours for one month sprints
Inputs: previous working product increment and knowledge about it.
Outputs: exchanged/shared knowledge as feedback.
Rules:
The meeting is focused on the required agenda defined by Scrum Master.
Sprint Retrospective:
Activities: introspect and improve within the Scrum process framework so as to make the next Sprint outcome more effective.
Duration: one hour meeting for two-week duration sprints and a three hour meeting for one month duration Sprints
Inputs: Team knowledge about last Sprint.
Outputs: Lesson learn and improvements plan.
Rules:
n/a
Sprint Planning:
Activities: plan work to be performed in the Sprint. Focus on questions:
What needs to be and can be delivered in the Sprint Increment?
How will the work needed for the execution of Sprint be achieved?
Steps are:
Scrum Team discusses the functionality that can be developed during the Sprint.
Product Owner provides clarifications on the Product Backlog items.
The team selects the items from the Product Backlog for the Sprint, as they are the best to assess what they can accomplish in the Sprint.
Duration: maximum of four hours (two weeks sprints) or eight hours (one month Sprints).
Inputs:
The Product Backlog
The latest product Increment
Projected capacity of the Team during the Sprint
Past performance of the Team
Outputs: Sprint Backlog
Product Backlog items selected for this Sprint
Delivering plan that aims two questions:
1. Why it is building the Product Increment?
2. How to build the selected functionality into a working product Increment?
Rules:
Work is divided into tasks of duration of one day or less.
The work is carried out in a collaborative fashion.
Work during a sprint is estimated during sprint planning and may be of varying size and/or effort.
Artifacts:
Product Backlog:
Description:
An ordered list of features that are needed as part of the end product.
Single source of requirements for any changes to be made to the product.
An evolving artifact, the earliest version of it may contain only the initially known and best understood requirements.
The Product Backlog gets developed as the product, and the environment in which it will be used, progress.
As the product being built is used and gains value, the Product Backlog becomes a larger and more exhaustive list. Changes in business requirements, market conditions, or technology, cause changes in the Product Backlog, making it a live artifact.
Multiple-ordered characteristic: Higher-ordered Product Backlog items are usually clearer and more detailed than lower-ordered ones. The lower the order, the lesser is the detail.
Content: features, functions, requirements, enhancements, and fixes
Attributes:
User story: description, order, estimate, and value
Rules:
The Product Owner is responsible for the Product Backlog, including its content, availability, and ordering.
Product Backlog items that may likely be the candidate requirements for the upcoming Sprint could be refined so that these items can be developed during the Sprint.
Product Backlog items that can be developed by the Team within one Sprint are deemed to be ready for selection in a Sprint planning meeting.
Sprint Backlog:
Description:
Set of Product Backlog items selected for the Sprint, plus a plan for delivering the product Increment and realizing the Sprint Goal.
The Sprint Backlog is a highly visible, real-time picture of the work that the Team plans to accomplish during the Sprint, and it belongs solely to the Team.
Content:
What functionality will be made available in the next Increment.
Work needed to deliver that functionality as a working product Increment.
Attributes: n/a
Rules:
Team forecasts Sprint Backlog.
During a Sprint, only the Team could modify the Sprint Backlog throughout the Sprint, and the Sprint Backlog could emerge during the Sprint.
As new work is required, the Team adds it to the Sprint Backlog.
As work is performed or completed, the estimated remaining work is updated.
When elements of the plan are deemed unnecessary, they are removed.
Increment:
Description: Sum of all the Product Backlog items completed during a Sprint combined with the increments of all previous Sprints.
Content: releasable software results from integrating this increment with releasable software of previous Sprint.
Attributes: n/a
Rules:
At the end of a Sprint, the new Increment must be a working product, which means it must be in a useable condition. It must be in working condition regardless of whether the Product Owner decides to actually release it.
Scrum Team needs to have consensus on what is considered to be an Increment. This is used to assess when work is complete on the product Increment.
Scrum Team must define a definition of Increment appropriate for the product.
Each Increment is additive to all prior Increments and thoroughly tested, ensuring that all Increments work together.
Sprint Burn-Down Chart:
Description:
Sprint Burn-Down Chart is a practice for trending the work expended by the Scrum Team.
Content: (will be describled in another section)
Attributes: n/a
Rules:
At any point in time in a Sprint, the total work remaining in the Sprint Backlog can be summed. The Team tracks this total work remaining for every Daily Scrum to project the likelihood of achieving the Sprint Goal.
The Product Owner tracks this total work remaining at least every Sprint Review.
This information is shared with all stakeholders.
4. Tools
User Stories
Product Backlog is a live and evolving artifact. In Scrum projects, the Product Backlog is a list of User Stories. It is a tool support for describing product features. It is narrated from user perspective regarding what she or he wants to have rather that what system can do for him.
The User Story Structure
As a <Type of User>,
I want <To Perform Some Task>,
So that <I can achieve some goal/benefit/value.>
User Story Acceptance Criteria: define correctness of implementation of the user story.
Acceptance Criterion 1:
Given
<Context condition 1>, ... <Context condition n>
When
<Event trigger condition 1>, ... <Event trigger condition n>
Then
<Post ensure condition 1>, ... <Post ensure condition n>
Rules
Creating:
Product Owner initially creates.
Team members refine, add, modify or remove.
Managing:
User Stories are managed in the Product Backlog.
User Stories are ordered according to priority.
The most prioritized user stories are refined to granular level, while the least priority user stories are kept at a lesser detail level.
The user stories can be reprioritized at any time.
Burn-Down Chart
Burn-Down Chart shows the remaining effort in day-wise number of hours. It is a tool support for Sprint tracking.
The y-axis presents remain hours, the x-axis presents target day.
The ideal scenario is presented by a linear line downs from the total hours of Sprint to 0 (the blue line).
The scenario is usually slipped up because of delays (the orange line).
The real scenario is a curve to meet to deadline because of daily meeting plan to adapt.

Planning Poker
Planning Poker is a technique or tool support User Stories difficult estimation.
Characteristics:
Difficult scale: Fibonacci sequence (value is estimated in point)
Activities:
One of the team members is selected as the Moderator.
Moderator reads the description of the User Story.
Product Owner answers questions if estimators raise some ones.
Each estimator privately selects a card representing her or his estimate.
After all estimators have made a selection, all cards are simultaneously turned over and share estimation to team.
In the first round, it is very likely that the estimations vary. The high and low estimators explain the reason for their estimates.
The moderator leads a discuss to solve this with all estimators.
The process repeat regards the discuss result until the estimations are not so much vary.
Rules:
Estimator privately estimates user story.
Estimations vary is reduced to a common value.
